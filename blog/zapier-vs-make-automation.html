You need to connect your apps, kill repetitive tasks, and stop copying data between tools like it's 2015. The two heavyweights in the no-code automation tools space — Zapier and Make (formerly Integromat) — both promise to do exactly that, but they approach the problem in fundamentally different ways. Choosing the wrong one means either overpaying for simplicity you don't need or drowning in complexity that slows you down. This is the only Zapier vs Make comparison you need to read in 2026.

Quick Verdict / TL;DR

Zapier wins on speed of setup, app breadth, and ease of use — it's the right choice for teams that want automation working in minutes without a learning curve. Make wins on flexibility, visual power, and price-per-operation — it's the better pick for power users and teams building complex, multi-branch workflows. If you automate fewer than 10 simple workflows, go Zapier. If you build anything with conditional logic, data transformation, or high-volume operations, go Make.

What Are Zapier and Make?

Zapier: The Automation Standard

Zapier launched in 2012 and essentially created the modern no-code automation category. Its core concept is the "Zap" — a trigger-action pair that connects two or more apps. Someone fills out a form, Zapier creates a row in your spreadsheet, sends a Slack message, and adds the contact to your CRM. It's linear, intuitive, and deliberately simple.

Over the past few years, Zapier has expanded well beyond basic Zaps. The platform now includes Zapier Tables (a lightweight database), Zapier Interfaces (form and page builder), Zapier Chatbots, and — critically for 2026 — deeply integrated AI actions powered by large language models. Zapier has evolved from an integration tool into something closer to a no-code work platform. Its integration library now spans over 7,000 apps (company data), making it the largest in the category by a wide margin.

Make: The Visual Automation Powerhouse

Make (rebranded from Integromat in 2022) takes a different philosophy. Instead of linear trigger-action chains, Make gives you a visual canvas where you build "scenarios" — flowchart-style automations with branching paths, loops, iterators, error handlers, and routers. Think of it as the difference between writing a to-do list and drawing a process map.

Make currently supports over 2,000 app integrations (company data), and while that's significantly fewer than Zapier, it covers the vast majority of popular SaaS tools. Where Make differentiates itself is depth: its HTTP/webhook module lets you connect to virtually any API, and its data manipulation tools (filters, aggregators, text parsers, JSON/XML modules) give you granular control that Zapier only recently started matching.

Make also made significant moves in 2025, introducing Make AI Assistants, expanded team collaboration features, and a revamped execution engine that improved scenario reliability and speed (user report).

Key Features Compared

Workflow Builder

This is the defining difference in the Zapier vs Make debate. Zapier's editor is form-based: you select a trigger, configure it, add steps sequentially, and test. It's fast. Most people can build their first Zap in under 10 minutes. Zapier added "Paths" (conditional branches) and "Looping" in recent years, but branching still feels bolted on rather than native to the design.

Make's visual scenario builder is a drag-and-drop canvas. You place modules, draw connections, add routers for branching logic, and see the entire flow at a glance. For complex workflows — say, processing incoming emails, classifying them with AI, routing urgent ones to Slack, logging others to a database, and handling errors gracefully — Make's canvas is dramatically clearer. The tradeoff is a steeper learning curve. Expect to spend an afternoon learning Make's interface before you're productive.

AI Capabilities

Both platforms have leaned hard into AI in 2025-2026. Zapier offers AI-powered actions (summarize text, classify data, extract information) natively within Zaps and has integrated AI across its product suite including AI-assisted Zap building. Make introduced AI Assistants and offers modules for OpenAI, Anthropic, Google Gemini, and other providers, giving you more control over prompt engineering and model selection. Zapier's AI is more turnkey; Make's is more configurable.

Error Handling

Make has a clear advantage here. You can add dedicated error handler routes to any module, set retry logic, define break/resume/rollback/commit behaviors, and route failed operations to alternative paths. Zapier has improved its error handling — you now get autoreplay, error notifications, and basic retry — but it still doesn't offer the granular, per-step error management that Make provides.

App Integrations

Zapier: 7,000+ integrations. Make: 2,000+ integrations. On raw numbers, Zapier dominates. If you rely on niche or industry-specific tools, there's a higher chance Zapier has a native connector. That said, Make's HTTP module and webhook support mean you can connect to nearly anything with an API — it just requires more setup.

Data Handling & Transformation

Make treats data transformation as a first-class feature. Built-in functions for text, math, dates, arrays, and JSON/XML parsing are available in every module. You can reshape data mid-flow without workarounds. Zapier has improved here with Formatter steps (text, numbers, dates, lookup tables), but complex data manipulation often requires chaining multiple Formatter steps, which consumes additional tasks and adds clutter.

Pricing Breakdown

Pricing is where the Zapier vs Make conversation gets spicy — and where Make typically wins on value.

Note: Pricing for both platforms changes frequently. The figures below reflect publicly available plans as of early 2026. Always check current pricing on each platform's website before purchasing.

Zapier Pricing

  • Free plan: Limited tasks per month, single-step Zaps only
  • Professional plan: Multi-step Zaps, filters, Formatter — aimed at individuals
  • Team plan: Shared workspace, shared connections, premier support
  • Enterprise plan: Custom — advanced admin, SSO, SCIM, dedicated support

Zapier counts each action (trigger + each subsequent step) as a "task." High-volume workflows can burn through task allotments quickly, especially multi-step Zaps. Overages get expensive.

Make Pricing

  • Free plan: Limited operations per month, basic features
  • Core plan: More operations, access to most modules
  • Pro plan: Full-featured, priority execution, custom variables
  • Teams plan: Team collaboration, roles, shared scenarios
  • Enterprise plan: Custom — SSO, audit logs, dedicated infrastructure

Make counts "operations" — each module execution within a scenario counts as one. The key difference: Make's operations are significantly cheaper per unit than Zapier's tasks, often by a factor of 4–10x depending on the plan tier (industry data). For high-volume automation, this cost gap is substantial.

Pros and Cons

Zapier Pros

  • Unmatched app library — 7,000+ integrations means you'll rarely hit a dead end
  • Fastest time-to-automation — build and deploy simple workflows in minutes
  • Growing platform play — Tables, Interfaces, Chatbots create an ecosystem
  • Excellent documentation and templates — thousands of pre-built Zap templates
  • AI assistant — describe what you want in plain English and get a working Zap

Zapier Cons

  • Expensive at scale — task-based pricing punishes high-volume and multi-step workflows
  • Limited branching and looping — Paths work but feel constrained compared to Make's routers
  • Weaker error handling — no per-module error routes or advanced retry logic
  • Data transformation is clunky — Formatter steps add tasks and visual noise
  • Vendor lock-in risk — reliance on Zapier Tables/Interfaces ties you deeper into one ecosystem

Make Pros

  • Visual workflow canvas — best-in-class for complex, branching automations
  • Superior error handling — per-module error routes, retries, and break/resume logic
  • Cost-effective at scale — operations are dramatically cheaper than Zapier tasks
  • Powerful data transformation — built-in functions eliminate the need for extra steps
  • HTTP/API flexibility — connect to virtually any service, even without a native module

Make Cons

  • Steeper learning curve — the visual builder is powerful but intimidating for newcomers
  • Fewer native integrations — 2,000+ is good, but you may need to build custom HTTP connections for niche tools
  • Scenario debugging can be tedious — tracing data through complex scenarios requires patience
  • Smaller template library — fewer pre-built scenarios to start from
  • Less polished UX — the interface has improved significantly but still doesn't feel as refined as Zapier

Who Should Use Which?

Choose Zapier If:

  • You're a solo operator, small team, or non-technical founder who needs automations running today
  • Your workflows are mostly linear — trigger, then do A, B, C in sequence
  • You rely on niche or long-tail SaaS tools that may not have Make integrations
  • You want a broader platform (Tables, Interfaces, Chatbots) rather than just an automation tool
  • You value templates and guided setup over raw flexibility

Choose Make If:

  • You're a power user, agency, or ops team building complex, multi-branch workflows
  • You process high volumes of data and need cost-efficient operations
  • Your workflows involve conditional logic, loops, error handling, or data transformation
  • You're comfortable with (or willing to learn) a more technical interface
  • You need to connect to APIs that don't have pre-built connectors

Final Verdict

Here's our honest take after years of testing both platforms: Make is the better automation tool for most serious use cases in 2026.

That's not a dismissal of Zapier — it's an acknowledgment that the automation landscape has matured. The people searching for "Zapier vs Make" in 2026 are mostly past the "I want to connect my form to my spreadsheet" phase. They need branching, error handling, data transformation, and cost efficiency. Make delivers all of that at a lower price point.

But context matters. If your team is non-technical, if you need 50 simple Zaps connecting common tools, or if you're buying into an all-in-one work platform, Zapier remains the more pragmatic choice. It's the automation tool that gets out of your way.

The best move? Take advantage of both free plans. Build the same workflow in each tool. You'll know within 30 minutes which one fits your brain and your budget.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Make really cheaper than Zapier?

In most scenarios, yes — often significantly so. Make's per-operation cost is substantially lower than Zapier's per-task cost, and Make's operations tend to be more granular, meaning a single "Zap run" in Zapier might correspond to fewer billed operations in Make than you'd expect. For high-volume workflows (thousands of runs per month), teams regularly report 50–80% cost savings after switching to Make (user report). However, the exact savings depend on your specific workflows, volume, and plan tier. Always check current pricing and do the math for your use case.

Can I migrate my Zaps from Zapier to Make?

There's no one-click migration tool between the platforms. You'll need to rebuild your workflows manually in Make. The good news: Make's community and documentation include guides for recreating common Zapier patterns, and many users report that the process forces them to optimize workflows they'd been neglecting.
