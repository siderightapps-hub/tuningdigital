Notion vs Obsidian for PKM

You've got hundreds of notes scattered across apps, bookmarks rotting in browser tabs, and a growing suspicion that your "system" for capturing knowledge isn't really a system at all. You need a personal knowledge management (PKM) tool that actually works with your brain — not against it. The two names that dominate every conversation about this are Notion and Obsidian, and they represent fundamentally different philosophies about how you should organize your thinking. Choosing wrong means months of migrating notes later, so let's get this right the first time.

Quick Verdict / TL;DR

Notion is the better choice if you want an all-in-one workspace that handles project management, databases, wikis, and collaboration alongside your notes. Obsidian wins if you prioritize long-term data ownership, speed, offline access, and building a deeply interconnected personal knowledge base. For solo PKM purists, pick Obsidian. For teams and people who want one app to replace five, pick Notion.

What Is Notion?

Notion is a cloud-based workspace that combines note-taking, databases, project management, and wikis into a single application. Launched in 2016 and steadily growing into one of the most popular productivity tools on the market — with over 30 million users (company-reported, 2023) — it positions itself as the "all-in-one workspace" that can replace your docs, spreadsheets, task boards, and knowledge base simultaneously.

At its core, Notion uses a block-based editor where everything — text, images, tables, embeds, toggles, callouts — is a draggable, nestable block. Pages can contain sub-pages infinitely, and databases can display information as tables, boards, calendars, timelines, galleries, or lists. It's extraordinarily flexible, which is both its greatest strength and its steepest learning curve.

Notion stores everything in the cloud. Your data lives on Notion's servers, you access it through web, desktop, or mobile apps, and real-time collaboration is baked in. This makes it excellent for teams but introduces a hard dependency on internet connectivity and Notion's continued existence as a company.

Notion AI

Notion has invested heavily in Notion AI, an integrated assistant that can summarize pages, generate content, extract action items, answer questions about your workspace, and autofill database properties. It's one of the more thoughtful AI integrations in the productivity space — it works within your existing content rather than feeling bolted on.

What Is Obsidian?

Obsidian is a local-first, Markdown-based knowledge management application built around the concept of linked thinking. Created by Shida Li and Erica Xu (who previously worked on Dynalist), it launched in 2020 and has built a fiercely loyal user base, particularly among researchers, developers, writers, and PKM enthusiasts.

The fundamental difference: Obsidian stores your notes as plain Markdown files in a folder on your computer. There's no proprietary format, no cloud lock-in, no database sitting between you and your words. If Obsidian disappeared tomorrow, you'd still have every note, perfectly readable in any text editor. For people who've been burned by apps shutting down or changing terms, this is not a minor detail — it's the entire point.

Obsidian's killer feature is bidirectional linking. You connect notes to each other using [[wiki-style links]], and over time, a web of interconnected ideas emerges. The Graph View visualizes these connections as an interactive node map, letting you see clusters of related thinking and discover unexpected relationships between ideas.

The Plugin Ecosystem

Obsidian's community plugin ecosystem is massive — over 1,800 community plugins at the time of writing (user report). You can turn Obsidian into a task manager, a daily journaling app, a Zettelkasten system, a spaced repetition flashcard tool, or a full publishing platform. This extensibility means the tool can be shaped precisely to your workflow, but it also means you'll spend time configuring it.

Key Features Compared

Editor and Writing Experience

Obsidian's editor is fast. Blazingly, noticeably fast — even with vaults containing tens of thousands of notes. It supports Markdown natively, which means your formatting travels with your files. The Live Preview mode gives you a clean writing experience without sacrificing the precision of Markdown syntax. For writers and thinkers who live in text, it feels like a power tool.

Notion's block editor is more visually polished and beginner-friendly. You can drag blocks around, create columns, embed media, and build visually rich pages without touching any syntax. But it's slower — noticeably so in large workspaces — and the editor sometimes fights you on simple things like indentation and text selection. Notion's editor optimizes for presentation; Obsidian's optimizes for speed and precision.

Organization and Structure

Notion gives you databases with properties, filters, sorts, relations, and rollups. You can create a content calendar, a CRM, a reading list, and a habit tracker — all as different views of structured data. This is where Notion genuinely has no equal among note-taking apps. If your PKM system needs structured, queryable data, Notion handles it natively.

Obsidian relies on a combination of folders, tags, links, and metadata (YAML frontmatter) for organization. The Dataview community plugin adds database-like querying capabilities, but it requires learning a query syntax and doesn't match the polish of Notion's databases. Obsidian excels at organic, networked organization — letting structure emerge from connections rather than being imposed top-down.

Collaboration

This one isn't close. Notion was built for teams. Real-time co-editing, commenting, mentions, permissions, shared workspaces — it's all there and it works well. Obsidian is fundamentally a single-player tool. While Obsidian Sync can technically share vaults, it's not designed for simultaneous collaboration. If you need to work with other people in the same knowledge base, Notion wins by default.

Offline and Performance

Obsidian works entirely offline. No internet? No problem. Every note is on your machine, every search is local, and the app opens in under a second. Notion has improved its offline capabilities, but it's still fundamentally cloud-dependent. Pages need to be cached individually, search requires connectivity for full results, and there's a perceptible lag when navigating large workspaces. If you work on planes, in cafes with spotty wifi, or simply value speed, Obsidian's local-first architecture is a tangible advantage.

Data Portability and Ownership

Obsidian: Your notes are plain .md files. You own them completely. Move them to another app, version-control them with Git, back them up however you want. This is the gold standard for data ownership.

Notion: You can export to Markdown or CSV, but the export is lossy — databases lose their relations, embedded content doesn't always survive, and the file structure can be messy. You're not locked in per se, but migration has real friction.

"I moved from Notion to Obsidian after three years, and the export process took me an entire weekend of cleanup. The actual notes were fine, but all my database relations, rollups, and linked views were just gone. If I'd started in Obsidian with Markdown files, I'd never have had that problem."

— Reddit user in r/ObsidianMD, 2024

Pricing Breakdown

Note: Pricing for both tools changes periodically. We recommend checking current pricing on each tool's official site before making a decision.

Notion Pricing

  • Free plan: Generous for individuals — unlimited pages and blocks, limited file uploads, and 7-day page history
  • Plus plan: Adds unlimited file uploads, 30-day page history, and more guests
  • Business plan: Advanced permissions, SAML SSO, 90-day page history
  • Enterprise: Custom pricing with advanced security and compliance features
  • Notion AI: Available as an add-on per member, per month across all plans

Obsidian Pricing

  • Personal use: Completely free, forever. No feature restrictions, no note limits, no catch
  • Commercial use: Requires a one-time license per user for business contexts
  • Obsidian Sync: Optional paid add-on for end-to-end encrypted syncing across devices
  • Obsidian Publish: Optional paid add-on for publishing notes as a website

For individual PKM use, Obsidian is functionally free. You can sync notes using iCloud, Google Drive, Dropbox, or Git without paying anything. Notion's free tier is also solid for individuals, but power users will likely hit limitations that push them toward a paid plan — especially with AI features gated behind an add-on.

Pros and Cons

Notion — Pros

  • All-in-one workspace replaces multiple tools
  • Powerful databases with multiple views and relations
  • Excellent real-time collaboration
  • Beautiful templates and a polished UI
  • Strong AI integration with Notion AI
  • Broad third-party integrations (Slack, Google Drive, Figma, etc.)

Notion — Cons

  • Cloud-dependent — offline mode is limited
  • Performance degrades in large workspaces
  • Data portability is mediocre — exports are lossy
  • Editor can feel sluggish compared to native apps
  • Complexity can lead to over-engineering your system
  • Privacy-conscious users may dislike data being stored on Notion's servers

Obsidian — Pros

  • Lightning-fast performance, even with huge vaults
  • Full data ownership with plain Markdown files
  • Works completely offline
  • Incredible plugin ecosystem for customization
  • Bidirectional linking and Graph View support networked thinking
  • Free for personal use with no meaningful restrictions
  • End-to-end encrypted sync available

Obsidian — Cons

  • No real-time collaboration
  • Steeper learning curve, especially with plugins
  • Database functionality requires plugins and workarounds
  • Mobile apps are functional but not as polished as Notion's
  • No native web app — requires installation
  • Can become a "productivity rabbit hole" of configuration and tinkering

Best For: Who Should Use Which?

Choose Notion If You...

  • Work with a team and need shared workspaces with real-time editing
  • Want one tool to handle notes, tasks, projects, wikis, and databases
  • Prefer a visual, low-code interface over Markdown syntax
  • Need structured data — CRMs, content calendars, inventory tracking, etc.
  • Want built-in AI assistance without third-party plugins
  • Don't mind cloud-dependent workflows

Choose Obsidian If You...

  • Are building a long-term personal knowledge base (Zettelkasten, digital garden, research vault)
  • Care deeply about data ownership and portability
  • Want an app that works offline without compromise
  • Prefer speed and keyboard-driven workflows
  • Are a developer, researcher, or writer who thinks in interconnected ideas
  • Want to customize your tool extensively through plugins
  • Are privacy-conscious and want local or end-to-end encrypted storage

Or Use Both

This isn't a cop-out — it's increasingly common. Many power users run Obsidian as their personal thinking and writing tool while using Notion for team collaboration and project management. The two tools occupy different niches, and there's no rule that says you can only pick one. The key is being intentional about what goes where.

Final Verdict

If you're choosing the best note-taking app for personal knowledge management specifically, Obsidian is the stronger choice. Its local-first architecture, plain-text file storage, speed, and linking capabilities make it purpose-built for the long game of accumulating and connecting knowledge. Your notes will outlast the app, and that matters when you're building something you want to use for decades.

If your needs extend beyond pure PKM into team collaboration, project management, and structured databases — and you want a single tool to handle all of it — Notion is the more practical choice. It does more things competently than almost any other productivity app on the market, and its AI features are genuinely useful, not just a marketing checkbox.

The Notion vs Obsidian debate ultimately comes down to philosophy: Do you want a powerful platform, or do you want a powerful tool? Notion is the platform — feature-rich, polished, team-ready, and cloud-native. Obsidian is the tool — fast, private, extensible, and built to get out of your way. Neither is objectively better. But one of them is almost certainly better for you
