Notion vs Obsidian for PKM

📅 26 May 2026 ✍️ Alex Bacsa 🔄 Updated regularly
Disclosure: Some links in this article are affiliate links. If you purchase through them, Tuning Digital may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. We only recommend tools we've genuinely tested and believe in. Our editorial opinions are never influenced by affiliate partnerships.

You've probably spent more time researching note-taking apps than actually taking notes. No judgement — I've been there. The Notion vs Obsidian debate has become the defining rivalry in personal knowledge management (PKM), and it's not a simple one to resolve because these tools approach the same problem from fundamentally different philosophies. One wants to be your everything app; the other wants to disappear and leave you alone with your markdown files. Choosing wrong means migrating thousands of notes later, so let's get this right the first time.

Quick Verdict

Obsidian is the better pure PKM tool for individuals who want to own their data, think in linked notes, and build a long-term knowledge base. Notion wins if you need collaboration, project management, and databases in the same workspace. The single most important factor: do you work alone or with a team?

  • Best for: Solo researchers and writers who prioritise data ownership → Obsidian. Teams and operators who need an all-in-one workspace → Notion.
  • Avoid if: You need real-time team collaboration — skip Obsidian. You want offline-first, local-file PKM — skip Notion.
  • Pricing from: Obsidian is free for personal use; Notion has a free tier with limits — check current pricing on each vendor's site.

What Are Notion and Obsidian, Really?

Notion launched in 2016 and has grown into one of the most recognisable productivity brands on the internet. It's a cloud-first workspace that combines notes, databases, wikis, kanban boards, and (more recently) AI features into a single platform. Think of it as a modular Lego set for organising your work life. Companies like Figma, Duolingo, and Nike have adopted it for internal wikis and project tracking, according to Notion's customer page.

Obsidian is different in almost every way that matters. Built by the team behind Dynalist, it stores everything as plain markdown files in a local folder on your device. There's no proprietary database, no server you depend on. Your notes are just .md files sitting in a directory. You could open them in Notepad if Obsidian vanished tomorrow. That philosophy of data ownership has built a fiercely loyal community since the app's public launch in 2020.

The philosophical gap here is real and it shapes everything else. Notion is a platform. Obsidian is a tool. Platforms give you more out of the box but lock you in. Tools give you freedom but demand more from you upfront.

Key Features Compared

Note-Taking and Editing

Notion uses a block-based editor. Every paragraph, image, toggle, or embed is a draggable block. It's intuitive if you've used something like WordPress's Gutenberg editor, but it can feel sluggish when you're trying to write quickly. I've noticed lag when working on pages with 50+ blocks, and others have reported the same.

Obsidian is a markdown editor at heart. You write in plain text with formatting syntax, or you use the live preview mode that renders headings, bold text, and links as you type. It's fast. Genuinely fast. I tested both apps with a 12,000-word client brief and Obsidian handled it without a flicker. Notion chugged.

Linking and Knowledge Graphs

This is where Obsidian flexes hardest. Bidirectional linking — where linking Page A to Page B automatically creates a backlink on Page B — is core to the app. The graph view lets you visualise your entire vault as an interconnected web of ideas. For Zettelkasten practitioners or anyone building a second brain, it's brilliant.

Notion has backlinks too, added in 2021. They work. But they feel bolted on rather than foundational. You won't find a graph view, and the linking experience doesn't have the same fluidity. If PKM linking is your primary use case, Obsidian wins this category decisively.

Databases and Project Management

Notion's databases are its killer feature. Filtered views, relations between tables, rollups, formulas — you can build a lightweight CRM, a content calendar, or a reading tracker without writing a line of code. For teams weighing up project management options, Notion often competes with dedicated tools; our comparison of Monday and Asana for project management covers that adjacent decision in detail.

Obsidian has no native database. Community plugins like Dataview let you query your notes using a SQL-like syntax, which is powerful but requires a willingness to learn. It's not a visual, drag-and-drop experience.

AI Features

Notion rolled out Notion AI as a paid add-on, offering summarisation, writing assistance, autofill for database properties, and Q&A across your workspace. It's genuinely useful for teams generating lots of documentation. We've done a deep dive in our Notion AI review for 2026 if you want the full picture.

Obsidian's AI story is community-driven. Plugins like Copilot and Smart Connections bring LLM capabilities into the app, often connecting to OpenAI or local models. The flexibility is impressive — you can run a fully local AI setup if data privacy matters to you — but nothing works out of the box.

Offline Access and Data Ownership

Obsidian works offline by default. Full stop. Your files live on your machine. You can sync them however you like: iCloud, Dropbox, Git, or Obsidian's own paid Sync service.

Notion requires an internet connection for most tasks. There's an offline mode, but it's limited and has been a long-standing pain point. The Verge reported on Notion's improved offline capabilities in 2024, and it's better than it was, but it's still not truly offline-first. If you regularly work on trains, planes, or anywhere with spotty Wi-Fi, this matters.

Extensibility

Both tools have plugin ecosystems, but they're different in scale and kind. Obsidian's community plugin library has over 1,800 plugins — everything from spaced repetition flashcards to Vim keybindings. The customisation ceiling is extraordinarily high.

Notion's integrations lean toward third-party services: Slack, Google Drive, Zapier, and its own API. It connects outward rather than letting you reshape the tool itself.

Pricing Breakdown

Obsidian is free for personal use. You pay nothing for the core app. The optional paid services are Sync (end-to-end encrypted sync across devices) and Publish (turning notes into a public website). Commercial use requires a paid licence. Check the Obsidian pricing page for current rates.

Notion offers a free plan with a 5MB file upload limit per file and limited block storage for teams. The Plus plan removes most restrictions. Business and Enterprise tiers add admin controls, SAML SSO, and advanced permissions. Notion AI is an additional per-member cost on top of any plan. Pricing has shifted multiple times, so verify on Notion's site directly.

For a solo user doing pure PKM, Obsidian is meaningfully cheaper — potentially £0 if you sync via iCloud or Dropbox. Notion's free tier works for light personal use, but teams will hit the paywall quickly.

Pros and Cons

Notion

  • Pro: All-in-one workspace — notes, databases, wikis, and project boards in one place
  • Pro: Excellent real-time collaboration with comments, mentions, and permissions
  • Pro: Polished, visual interface with low learning curve for basic use
  • Pro: Native AI features that work across your entire workspace
  • Con: Cloud-dependent; limited offline functionality
  • Con: Performance degrades on large or deeply nested pages
  • Con: Data portability is weak — exporting to markdown loses formatting and database structure
  • Con: Pricing adds up for teams, especially with AI

Obsidian

  • Pro: Local-first, plain markdown files — total data ownership
  • Pro: Blazing fast, even with vaults containing thousands of notes
  • Pro: Best-in-class linking, backlinks, and graph view for PKM
  • Pro: Massive plugin ecosystem with deep customisation
  • Pro: Free for personal use
  • Con: No real-time collaboration (you can share vaults, but it's not designed for teams)
  • Con: Steeper learning curve, especially with plugins and CSS customisation
  • Con: No native databases — workarounds exist but require effort
  • Con: Mobile app is functional but less polished than Notion's

Who Is Each Tool Best For?

Choose Notion if you are…

A team leader, startup founder, or project manager who needs a shared workspace that does more than just notes. Notion shines when you're building a company wiki, tracking tasks across a team, or managing a content pipeline. It's also strong for freelancers who want one tool for client projects, invoicing databases, and meeting notes.

If your PKM needs are secondary to your operational needs — say you want a reading list alongside your sprint board and team docs — Notion handles that breadth well. It's the Swiss Army knife. Good at many things, exceptional at being a single pane of glass.

Students also gravitate toward Notion for its templates and visual organisation style. The free Education plan (available with a university email) is generous.

Choose Obsidian if you are…

A researcher, writer, developer, or lifelong learner who treats note-taking as thinking. If you've read Sönke Ahrens's How to Take Smart Notes or you follow the Zettelkasten method, Obsidian was essentially built for you.

It's also the right choice if data sovereignty matters. Journalists, lawyers, therapists, and anyone handling sensitive material should think carefully about where their notes live. With Obsidian, they live on your device, encrypted however you choose. Under UK GDPR, that local-first approach simplifies your data processing obligations considerably.

And if you're the type who enjoys tinkering — customising CSS, writing templates, building plugin workflows — Obsidian rewards that investment. It grows with you in a way Notion can't match.

If you're exploring the wider landscape of alternatives, our roundup of the best Notion alternatives in 2026 covers several other options worth considering alongside Obsidian.

Final Verdict

There isn't a single best note-taking app. There's the best app for how you work.

Obsidian is the superior personal knowledge management tool. Its linking, speed, privacy model, and extensibility make it the clear choice for individuals building a long-term knowledge base. If I had to recommend one tool for solo PKM in 2026, it would be Obsidian without much hesitation.

Notion is the superior workspace. If your notes need to coexist with databases, team collaboration, and project tracking, Notion delivers a cohesive experience that Obsidian simply doesn't try to replicate. It's a different category of tool wearing the same "note-taking" label.

My honest recommendation? Try both for a week with real work, not toy examples. Create 20 genuine notes in each. The one that feels like less friction after seven days is your answer.

Best for solo PKM and data ownership: Obsidian.

Best for team collaboration and all-in-one workflows: Notion.

Avoid Obsidian if: you need your team editing the same workspace in real time.

Avoid Notion if: you want guaranteed offline access and full control over your files.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Obsidian really free?

Yes, Obsidian is free for personal and non-commercial use. You only pay if you want their optional Sync or Publish services, or if you use it commercially.

Can I use Notion offline?

Notion has a limited offline mode that caches recently visited pages, but it's not designed for extended offline use. If reliable offline access is essential, Obsidian is the stronger choice.

Is it easy to switch from Notion to Obsidian?

You can export Notion pages as markdown, but the process is imperfect. Database views, relations, and embedded content often break during export, so the more complex your Notion setup, the harder migration becomes.

Which is better for students?

Notion's free Education plan and visual templates make it popular with students who want organisation alongside notes. Obsidian is better for students focused on deep research, literature reviews, or building a permanent knowledge base across years of study.

Does Obsidian have AI features?

Not natively, but community plugins connect Obsidian to AI models including OpenAI's GPT and local LLMs. This gives you flexibility but requires manual setup compared to Notion's built-in AI.

Which app is more secure for sensitive data?

Obsidian stores files locally on your device, giving you full control over encryption and access. Notion stores data on its cloud servers. For handling sensitive or regulated data under UK GDPR, Obsidian's local-first model is generally simpler to manage from a compliance standpoint.
