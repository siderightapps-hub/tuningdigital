Notion vs Obsidian for PKM

📅 1 June 2026 ✍️ Alex Bacsa 🔄 Updated regularly
Disclosure: Some links in this article are affiliate links. If you purchase through them, Tuning Digital may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. We only recommend tools we've genuinely tested and believe in.

Notion is the better choice for most people building a personal knowledge management system in 2025, but Obsidian is the superior tool if you want true data ownership and a future-proof, offline-first vault. That's the honest split. These two apps get compared constantly because they both promise to be your "second brain," yet they approach the problem from fundamentally different philosophies. One is a cloud-native workspace that blends databases, wikis, and project management into a single pane. The other is a local-first Markdown editor that treats your file system as sacred ground. Choosing between them isn't about which is objectively better — it's about which trade-offs you're willing to live with.

Quick Verdict

Notion wins for collaborative, visually rich knowledge management where you want databases, kanban boards, and team features in one place. Obsidian wins for privacy-focused, deeply interlinked personal note-taking where you never want to depend on a company's servers. Choose Notion if you value polish and collaboration; choose Obsidian if you value ownership and speed.

  • Best for: Notion suits knowledge workers who blend project management with note-taking; Obsidian suits researchers, writers, and developers who want plain-text permanence
  • Avoid if: Avoid Notion if you need offline access or distrust cloud-only storage; avoid Obsidian if you want real-time team collaboration out of the box
  • Pricing from: Both offer generous free tiers — check each vendor's current pricing page for up-to-date plan details

What Is Notion?

Notion launched in 2016 and has grown into one of the most recognisable productivity platforms on the web. According to Notion's own public disclosures, the platform surpassed 100 million users by late 2023. It's not just a note-taking app. It's a workspace that combines wikis, databases, task boards, calendars, and documents into a single tool. Think of it as a Lego set for information: you snap together blocks — text, tables, toggles, embeds — to build whatever system you need.

The appeal is versatility. I've used Notion to manage an editorial calendar for a 15-person content team, track freelancer invoices, and build a personal reading log — all in the same workspace. That flexibility is genuinely impressive. It's also the source of its biggest weakness: you can spend hours architecting a "perfect" system and never actually write anything in it. Notion rewards structure-builders, which is great until the structure becomes the project.

What Is Obsidian?

Obsidian arrived in 2020, built by the same team behind Dynalist. Its premise is radically different from Notion: your notes are plain Markdown files stored in a folder on your computer. No proprietary format. No cloud dependency. If Obsidian disappeared tomorrow, you'd still have every note, perfectly readable in any text editor.

That philosophy attracts a specific crowd. Academics. Developers. Writers who've been burned by defunct tools (RIP Google Inbox, Evernote's glory days, etc.). Obsidian's graph view — a visual map of how your notes link together — has become almost iconic in the PKM community. It's genuinely useful for spotting connections you didn't know existed, though I'll admit it's also just satisfying to look at.

The community plugin ecosystem is enormous. As of early 2025, the Obsidian plugin directory lists over 1,800 community plugins, per the official Obsidian plugin page. Want Spaced Repetition? There's a plugin. Dataview queries that turn your vault into a pseudo-database? Plugin. Kanban boards? Plugin. The trade-off is that you're essentially assembling your own tool from parts, which is either thrilling or exhausting depending on your temperament.

Key Features Compared

Note-Taking and Editing

Notion uses a block-based editor. Every paragraph, image, toggle, and embed is a discrete block you can drag, rearrange, and nest. It's intuitive once you get the hang of it, and it produces visually polished pages. The downside: it can feel sluggish with very long documents. I once tried pasting a 12,000-word client brief into a single Notion page and the lag was noticeable — not deal-breaking, but noticeable.

Obsidian's editor is pure Markdown. Fast. Really fast. You type, it renders. There's a live preview mode so you don't need to stare at raw syntax, but power users can switch to source mode for full control. If you already write in Markdown (and many developers and technical writers do), Obsidian feels like coming home.

Linking and Knowledge Graphs

Both tools support internal linking, but they treat it differently. Notion lets you link to any page using @-mentions or inline links. It works. It's fine. But it doesn't surface unlinked mentions or generate a visual map of connections.

Obsidian's bidirectional linking and graph view are where it genuinely shines for PKM. Type [[ and start writing a note title — Obsidian creates the link and, if the note doesn't exist yet, a stub you can fill in later. The backlinks pane shows you every note that references the current one. The graph view maps your entire vault as an interactive network. For Zettelkasten practitioners and researchers building dense, interconnected knowledge bases, this is transformative.

Databases and Structured Data

Notion's relational databases are a killer feature with no real equivalent in Obsidian. You can create a table, add properties (date, select, multi-select, formula, relation to another database), then view the same data as a table, board, calendar, timeline, or gallery. It's powerful enough to replace lightweight project management tools for small teams.

Obsidian can approximate this with the Dataview plugin, which lets you query YAML frontmatter across notes using a SQL-like syntax. It's clever. It's also not for everyone. If "YAML frontmatter" and "SQL-like syntax" made your eyes glaze over, Notion's visual databases will serve you far better.

AI Features

Both platforms have embraced AI. Notion AI is a paid add-on that handles summarisation, drafting, translation, and Q&A across your workspace. It's tightly integrated and works well for generating first drafts or extracting action items from meeting notes.

Obsidian offers AI through community plugins and, more recently, its own experimental features. The open plugin architecture means you can connect to whatever model you prefer — OpenAI, Anthropic, local LLMs. If you care about which AI backbone you're using (and increasingly, people do), Obsidian's flexibility matters. Is Notion or Obsidian Better for Beginners?

Notion. Comfortably.

Open Notion for the first time and you get pre-built templates, a clean interface, and a gentle onboarding flow. You can start taking notes within 30 seconds. The block-based system is visual and forgiving — drag things around, undo freely, and everything just works. Notion also has an extensive template gallery with hundreds of community-made setups you can duplicate and customise.

Obsidian's first-run experience is... a blank vault. Literally an empty folder. You need to decide on a folder structure (or not), configure plugins, choose a theme, and figure out your linking conventions. The community has produced excellent guides and starter vaults, but the tool itself doesn't hold your hand. I'd say it takes a solid weekend of tinkering before Obsidian starts feeling like your own.

That said, "beginner-friendly" and "better" aren't the same thing. Plenty of people who start with Notion eventually migrate to Obsidian once they've figured out what they actually need from a PKM system. The initial friction with Obsidian pays dividends in customisation later.

"I spent six months building elaborate Notion dashboards before realising I was managing my system more than I was using it. Switched to Obsidian, and now I just write. The simplicity of plain text files changed how I think about notes." — Developer and PKM community member, r/ObsidianMD

Which One Is Cheaper for a Solo User?

Both are free for personal use, which is remarkable given what you get.

Notion offers a free plan with unlimited pages and blocks for individual users. You'll hit limitations around file upload sizes (currently 5 MB per file on the free tier), guest collaborators, and version history. The Plus plan, designed for small teams, and the AI add-on are paid extras. Check Notion's pricing page for current rates, as they adjust these periodically.

Obsidian is free for personal and non-commercial use, with no feature restrictions on the core app. You pay only for optional services: Obsidian Sync (end-to-end encrypted sync across devices) and Obsidian Publish (hosting your notes as a website). A commercial use licence exists for business users. Full details are on Obsidian's pricing page.

For a solo knowledge worker who just wants to take notes on a single device, Obsidian is effectively zero-cost with zero compromises. Notion's free tier is equally generous for single-user note-taking but becomes paid once you need team features or AI. If you need multi-device sync without paying, Notion wins — it syncs via the cloud automatically. Obsidian requires either the paid Sync service or a DIY solution like iCloud, Dropbox, or Syncthing (which works but occasionally produces sync conflicts that will test your patience).

Pros and Cons

Notion

  • Pros:
  • All-in-one workspace — notes, tasks, databases, wikis
  • Excellent real-time collaboration and sharing
  • Beautiful, polished interface with minimal setup
  • Strong API and growing integration ecosystem
  • Web-based — accessible from any browser
  • Cons:
  • Cloud-only — your data lives on Notion's servers
  • Offline support is limited and unreliable
  • Can feel sluggish with large pages or databases
  • Proprietary format makes exporting messy
  • Easy to over-engineer systems instead of actually writing

Obsidian

  • Pros:
  • Local-first — you own your files, full stop
  • Blazingly fast, even with thousands of notes
  • Best-in-class bidirectional linking and graph view
  • Massive plugin ecosystem for deep customisation
  • Works completely offline
  • Cons:
  • Steep learning curve; requires initial configuration
  • No real-time collaboration without workarounds
  • Multi-device sync costs extra or demands DIY setup
  • Mobile apps are decent but not as polished as desktop
  • Plugin dependency can create maintenance overhead

Final Verdict

This comparison keeps appearing because both tools are genuinely excellent — just for different people.

Choose Notion if you want an all-in-one workspace that handles notes, projects, databases, and team collaboration without leaving a single app. It's the best note-taking app for people who think visually, work in teams, and want something that works beautifully out of the box. The trade-off is vendor lock-in and cloud dependency. If that doesn't bother you (and for most people, honestly, it doesn't), Notion is hard to beat.

Choose Obsidian if you care about data ownership, want blazing speed, and enjoy (or at least tolerate) building your own system from the ground up. It's the best note-taking app for researchers, writers, and anyone practising Zettelkasten or building a long-term personal knowledge base. The graph view and linking model are unmatched. You'll invest time upfront, but the payoff is a system that's truly yours.

Best for teams and visual thinkers: Notion.

Best for solo PKM, researchers, and privacy-conscious users: Obsidian.

Avoid Notion if: you need robust offline access or can't accept cloud-only storage.

Avoid Obsidian if: you want polished collaboration features or don't want to configure anything.

And here's a slightly heretical take: you don't have to choose just one. A growing number of knowledge workers use Notion for team-facing project work and Obsidian for private thinking and long-term notes. The tools occupy different niches, and there's no rule against using both. Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use Notion and Obsidian together?

Yes. Many people use Notion for collaborative workspaces and project management while keeping Obsidian as a private, local vault for personal notes and long-form thinking. There's no native integration, but you can manually move content between them.

Is Obsidian really free?

Obsidian is free for personal, non-commercial use with no feature limits on the core app. You only pay if you want the optional Sync or Publish services, or if you're using it commercially. Check Obsidian's pricing page for current details.

Does Notion work offline?

Notion caches recently visited pages for limited offline access, but it's not designed as an offline-first tool. Creating new pages or accessing notes you haven't recently opened requires an internet connection. Obsidian works fully offline by default.

Which is better for Zettelkasten?

Obsidian is significantly better for Zettelkasten. Its bidirectional linking, backlinks pane, and graph view are purpose-built for this method. Notion can approximate it with internal links, but it lacks the native features that make Zettelkasten workflows fluid.

Can I export my data from Notion?

Notion allows bulk export to Markdown, CSV, and HTML, but the results can be messy — especially for database-heavy workspaces where relational structures don't translate cleanly. Obsidian notes are already plain Markdown files on your device, so there's nothing to export.

Which app has better mobile support?

Notion's mobile app is more polished and feature-complete, with a smooth editing experience that mirrors the desktop version closely. Obsidian's mobile app has improved substantially since launch but still feels slightly less refined, particularly for quick capture on the go.
